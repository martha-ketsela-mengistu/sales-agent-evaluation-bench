# Synthesis Memo — Prometheus 2: An Open-Source Language Model Specialized in Evaluating Other Language Models
**Kim et al., 2024 (arXiv 2405.01535)**
**Written:** 2026-04-30 | **Author:** Martha Ketsela Mengistu

---

## Paper Summary

Kim et al. train Prometheus 2 by fine-tuning Mistral 7B on a preference dataset of evaluation judgments. The dataset, Preference Collection, contains 1,000 evaluation rubrics spanning 40+ domains, each with multiple candidate outputs and pairwise preference labels generated by GPT-4 and then verified. The training procedure uses DPO on (preferred judgment, dispreferred judgment) pairs — teaching the model not just to output scores but to prefer producing more accurate, rubric-grounded evaluation rationales. The key empirical finding is that Prometheus 2 achieves correlation with human evaluators comparable to GPT-4 on the Feedback Bench suite, while being open-weight and deployable locally. They attribute this to rubric-conditioned training: the model is always given an explicit rubric, preventing it from defaulting to surface-level preference signals like length and format.

## Application to Tenacious-Bench v0.1

### Where the paper's architecture is adapted

**Rubric-conditioned judge design.** Prometheus 2's central lesson — that rubric-conditioned evaluation outperforms holistic scoring — is directly implemented in `scoring_evaluator.py`. The LLM judge receives the full rubric array with per-dimension criteria text, anchors (1 = fail, 5 = exemplary), and weights. The judge is never asked to produce a holistic "how good is this output?" score; it is asked to score each of five dimensions independently using the supplied rubric. This design follows Prometheus 2's architecture directly.

**Small backbone with explicit evaluation signal.** Prometheus 2 trains a 7B model and achieves GPT-4-level evaluation correlation. This establishes that a small backbone fine-tuned with explicit evaluation data generalizes well on specialized rubrics. The Tenacious judge targets Qwen 3.5 0.8B–2B, a smaller backbone, but on a much narrower domain (six failure categories, five rubric dimensions). The narrower domain reduces the required generalization capacity, justifying the smaller backbone choice.

**Preference Collection as data construction template.** Prometheus 2's Preference Collection labels judgments by quality: a better judgment is one that is more rubric-adherent, less biased, and better calibrated to the reference answer. The Tenacious preference pairs follow an analogous construction: the chosen judgment is the one that correctly identifies the constraint violation; the rejected judgment is one that misses it. This directly adapts the Preference Collection paradigm to a binary constraint-detection task.

## Specific Disagreement: Prometheus 2's Training Signal Requires Explicit Judgment Rationales, Which Are Unavailable for Binary Constraint Tasks

Prometheus 2's training data consists of *judgment rationales* — GPT-4-generated explanations of why one output is better than another. The DPO objective is applied to pairs of rationales: the model learns to prefer producing the more accurate, rubric-grounded explanation. This is what makes Prometheus 2 a *reasoning evaluator* rather than a scoring function.

**The disagreement:** For Tenacious-Bench's constraint-detection judge, judgment rationales are not the primary training signal and may not be obtainable without significant cost.

The Tenacious judge is deployed as a pass/fail filter in a production pipeline. The output required is a binary decision (or a composite score above/below a threshold) — not a free-text rationale explaining why an output fails. Training on rationale pairs requires generating those rationales from a frontier model, which at 139 training tasks × N candidates per task would cost approximately $15–20 at Claude Sonnet 4.6 pricing — exceeding the week's API budget.

**The paper's own evidence qualifies this.** Kim et al. note in their ablation (Table 6) that a version of Prometheus 2 trained without explicit rationale generation, only on score-level preference signals, retains approximately 85% of the full model's correlation with human evaluators. This is the relevant data point: rationale training improves performance, but score-level preference training alone produces a usable judge at substantially lower data cost.

**Design decision:** The Tenacious judge is trained on score-level preference pairs only, not on free-text rationale pairs. Each preference pair consists of (chosen output with pass label, rejected output with fail label) plus the rubric text — no rationale is generated or trained on. This is a conscious departure from Prometheus 2's full procedure, justified by (a) cost constraints and (b) the binary detection task not requiring explanation capability. The ablation table will report whether adding a rationale-generation step on a 10% sample improves dev-set calibration, using the reserve budget allocation.

---

## Implication for Backbone Choice

Prometheus 2 uses Mistral 7B. The paper shows that a 7B backbone with 1,000 rubrics and ~100K training examples achieves strong generalization. Tenacious-Bench trains on 139 tasks (Path B, training partition), which is far fewer. This creates a data-to-model-capacity mismatch: a 7B model trained on 139 examples will overfit severely. Qwen 3.5 0.8B is the right backbone for this data scale — small enough that even 139 preference pairs provide meaningful regularization without overfitting. The Prometheus 2 paper's strong performance comes partly from data scale; at the 139-example scale, model capacity should be reduced proportionally.
