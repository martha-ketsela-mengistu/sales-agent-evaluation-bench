# Synthesis Memo — Preference Leakage: A Contamination Problem in LLM-as-a-Judge
**Li et al., 2025 (arXiv 2502.01534)**
**Written:** 2026-04-30 | **Author:** Martha Ketsela Mengistu

---

## Paper Summary

Li et al. identify a specific contamination failure mode in LLM-as-a-judge evaluation pipelines: when the same LLM (or same model family) is used to both generate training data and judge that data, the judge's preferences encode the generator's stylistic tendencies rather than the intended evaluation criterion. They call this *preference leakage*. Their controlled experiments show that a Claude-generated dataset judged by Claude-based evaluators inflates scores by 8–15% relative to cross-model evaluation; GPT-4-generated data judged by GPT-4 inflates by similar margins. The inflation is not from explicit memorization but from systematic stylistic alignment: the judge has internalized the generator's surface preferences (sentence structure, hedging patterns, formality register) and scores outputs matching that style more favorably — independent of actual rubric compliance.

Their proposed mitigations are: (1) use model families that are architecturally distinct for generation and judging; (2) run cross-model calibration on a fixed reference set; and (3) document the generation-judge model pairing in the dataset metadata so downstream users can assess leakage risk.

## Application to Tenacious-Bench v0.1

### Where the mitigation is implemented

**Model family rotation.** The multi-LLM synthesis pipeline uses three separate model families at distinct stages: frontier generation (OpenRouter GPT-class or Claude), bulk variation (DeepSeek V3.2 or Qwen3-Next-80B), and judge filtering (a third family, not the generator). In `generation_scripts/multi_llm_synthesis/`, the seed generator, bulk variation model, and judge filter model are configured separately in `router_config.json` and are never the same family. This implements Li et al.'s primary mitigation.

**Claude Sonnet 4.6 is the evaluator, not a generator.** The held-out evaluation uses Claude Sonnet 4.6 as the eval-tier judge. No tasks in the held-out partition were generated using Claude — synthesis used GPT-class models for seeds and Qwen/DeepSeek for variation. This cross-model separation prevents preference leakage from inflating held-out scores.

**Metadata logging.** Each task in `tenacious_bench_v0.1/` records the `source_mode` and, for multi-LLM synthesis tasks, the generation model family is traceable through `judge_filter_log.jsonl`. This enables downstream users to identify the generation-judge pairing, consistent with Li et al.'s documentation recommendation.

## Specific Disagreement: The Paper's Model-Family Rotation Criterion Is Underspecified for Instruct-Tuned Models Sharing Base Weights

Li et al.'s operationalization of "architecturally distinct model families" is: use different providers' models (e.g., Claude vs. GPT vs. Llama vs. DeepSeek). The implicit assumption is that different providers train on different corpora and align with different annotation pools, producing genuinely distinct preference functions.

**The disagreement:** This criterion breaks down for the growing class of open-weight models that are instruction-tuned from the same base (e.g., multiple RLHF variants of Llama 3, multiple fine-tunes of Qwen 3.5). Two models that share base pretraining weights will share lexical and stylistic preferences even if their instruction-tuning pipelines differ. If a Qwen 3.5 variant generates training data and a different Qwen 3.5 variant judges it, preference leakage may occur through the shared base — not through provider-level stylistic alignment, but through shared pretraining vocabulary and n-gram distributions.

**Evidence relevant to this project:** Tenacious-Bench training data uses OpenRouter cheap-tier models for preference pair construction (DeepSeek for rejected candidates, dev-tier rewrites for chosen outputs) and the Tenacious judge being *trained* is Qwen 3.5. This is a potential leakage vector if any training data was generated by Qwen-family models: the to-be-trained Qwen judge may systematically prefer Qwen-generated phrasing, not because of rubric compliance but because of shared pretraining.

**Mitigation applied:** Confirmed that the bulk variation stage uses DeepSeek V3.2, not Qwen-family models, and the chosen-output rewrites use a model from the generation config's `dev` tier (specified in `router_config.json`), which is also non-Qwen. The to-be-trained Qwen 3.5 judge therefore has no systematic exposure to Qwen-generated training examples, breaking the shared-base leakage vector. This is a stricter policy than Li et al.'s model-family rotation recommendation, and the reason for it is the shared-base risk they do not address.

## Implication for Held-Out Evaluation

Li et al.'s calibration recommendation — run cross-model comparison on a fixed reference set — applies directly to the held-out evaluation design. Delta A (trained Qwen judge vs. Week 10 Claude baseline) involves two different judge models scoring the same held-out tasks. If there is systematic preference divergence between the trained Qwen judge and Claude Sonnet 4.6, the reported lift may partly reflect model-family preference differences rather than training gains.

**Mitigation:** All held-out evaluation runs use Claude Sonnet 4.6 as the primary judge, consistent across baseline and ablation conditions. The trained Qwen judge is evaluated by having it produce pass/fail decisions on held-out tasks, and these are compared to Claude Sonnet 4.6's scores on the same tasks. Agreement between judge models is reported as a calibration metric in `ablation_results.json`. If inter-judge agreement on held-out tasks is below 0.80 kappa, the lift estimate is flagged as potentially confounded by model-family preference divergence.
